# Sensory presentation method

## Purpose

Use this reference when a deck should apply Kenya Hara-inspired design methodology without copying visual work. The method is upstream of style: it decides what to observe, what to reframe, and what viewers should feel before any layout is chosen.

## The five moves

1. **Ordinary observation** — Select one common object, action, space, or time fragment.
2. **Sensory resource** — Name a bodily or environmental quality: reflection, friction, waiting, weight, warmth, diffusion, translucency, residue, or silence.
3. **Reframe** — Write a one-sentence interpretation that makes the familiar newly visible.
4. **Material evidence** — Select a real surface, light condition, photo, diagram, or behavior that proves the reframe.
5. **Aftertaste** — End with a new way of noticing, not a decorative slogan.

These moves reflect Hara Design Institute's public treatment of WHITE as a sensory resource, HAPTIC as design originating in how people feel, and RE-DESIGN as discovering value in ordinary contexts.

## Brief template

```yaml
ordinary_object: ""
sensory_resource: ""
reframe: ""
material_cue: ""
audience_aftertaste: ""
evidence:
  - ""
motion_intent: ""
```

Reject the brief if `ordinary_object`, `sensory_resource`, or `reframe` are generic. “Innovation”, “AI”, “growth”, “minimalism”, and “future” are themes, not observations.

## Content sequence

| Page role | Question | Suitable page treatment |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Entry | What condition should the audience enter? | Quiet hero or object field |
| Observation | What familiar thing is normally overlooked? | Object/detail page |
| Detail | What exact feature deserves attention? | Close crop, material, time slice |
| Reframe | What new sentence changes the viewer's reading? | Large type statement |
| Evidence | Why is the reframe true or useful? | Data, comparison, quote, system map |
| Aftertaste | What should remain after the facts end? | Sparse close |

Do not lead with a metric if the audience has not yet felt why the metric matters.

## Material and motion mapping

| Sensory resource | Visual evidence | Motion if needed |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Translucency | layered paper, frosted surface, light | very slow light shift |
| Reflection | mirror, water, polished metal | gentle displaced reflection |
| Waiting | doorway, cursor, queue, dusk | delayed reveal or stillness |
| Friction | paper fiber, worn edge, hand mark | static texture; avoid animation by default |
| Accumulation | sediment, archive, trace, stack | staged reveal only when sequence matters |
| Warmth | skin tone, warm paper, close light | low-frequency tonal change |

Never use a generic fluid shader, particle field, or image merely as atmosphere. A moving hero must include `data-motion-intent` describing its content-specific role.

## Current editorial visual layer

The visual layer may retain: black/paper alternation, serif display type, mono metadata, high type contrast, precise grids, hero/non-hero rhythm, and one accent. These are delivery choices, not the method itself.

Keep the accent semantic. For example, use one accent to mark a changed state, a material trace, or a decision axis. Do not use two saturated colors simply because the template has them.

## Quality gates

### P0 — block delivery

- Every slide has an explicit sensory task.
- Every animated hero has a nonempty motion intent.
- Dynamic content has a static/reduced-motion alternative.
- Every image is evidence of object, material, place, or behavior; no embedded slide text.
- Every slide retains meaning when motion is disabled.
- Slide labels, navigation, and first/last page behavior work.

### P1 — revise before handoff

- Use at most one semantic accent per deck.
- Keep hero pages to one-third or fewer of all slides.
- Do not repeat one composition three times in a row.
- Give at least one page enough empty time/space to act as an entry or aftertaste.
- Limit a normal slide to one focal element and three supporting units.
- Use data, process, and comparison pages only after the reframe.
